  • 3D Printing Components for Low-Cost Plasma Devices

    Graves — plasma physics, materials

    Freshman, Sophomore, Junior, Senior — Course Credit — 5 hours per week

    Desired Skills: Linux, electronics basics, programming, 3D printing or other CAD experience

    Start: Immediately, Summer 2016

    Duration: ideally through the end of 2016

    This project will evaluate the feasibility of 3D printing components for the low-cost atmospheric pressure plasma systems that our lab is investigating for potential applications in materials processing and biomedicine. The researcher will work INDEPENDENTLY, with mentorship on plasma device design and performance metrics from Brandon, to design, manufacture, and test plasma devices using a top-of-the-line FormLabs Form2 stereolithographic 3D printer and the GravesLab test and measurement equipment. Ideally, the researcher will also: identify, test, and document a modeling and printing software toolchain that will use, whenever possible, free and open source software that will run on Linux; develop protocols for analyzing the mechanical, thermal, and electrical properties of 3D printed components; evaluate the performance and wear characteristics of different commercial resins for printing parts for dielectric barrier discharge and jet systems; prepare a literature review and assist in preparing a manuscript on ""frugal plasma"" systems.

    Please submit resume, cover letter, LinkedIn, description of something you've built or fixed recently to Brandon Curtis.

  • Control Systems for Plasma Devices

    Graves — plasma physics, computer science, electronics, process control

    Freshman, Sophomore, Junior, Senior — Course Credit — 5 hours per week

    Desired Skills: Linux, electronics, programming, embedded devices (Arduino, TI Launchpad)

    Start: Immediately, Summer 2016

    Duration: ideally through the end of 2016

    Design, build, program, and test systems for controlling the atmospheric pressure plasma devices that our lab is investigating for potential applications in materials processing and biomedicine. The researcher will work INDEPENDENTLY, with mentorship on plasma devices, electronics, and programming from Brandon, to explore ways of interfacing sensors to microcontroller prototyping boards like the Arduino and implementing algorithms to accomplish control goals with atmospheric pressure plasma devices. If the researcher enjoys pain, they may attempt to learn the basics of hardware design using field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs). SOME ASSEMBLY REQUIRED: This project is difficult and not for the faint of heart, but a good choice for someone (or a small team of 2-4) interested in an open-ended independent study to improve your DIY, programming, and electronics hacking skills.

    Please submit resume, cover letter, LinkedIn, description of something you've built or fixed recently to Brandon Curtis.
